The Cell Protocol

The formal rules behind a digital meitheal — how it works and where it's going.

Formal Paper (PDF)
Formal specification and roadmap · 15 pages

How it works

Connect Again is a digital meitheal — the Irish tradition where neighbours come together to help with the work that needs doing. You post what you can offer and what you need. When someone's interested, you agree on a credit amount (1–10 per exchange) and do the exchange in the real world. Both of you confirm it happened, and credits transfer automatically — from the person who received help to the person who gave it.

You start with 5 credits. Your balance can't go below −10. The community has room for up to 80 members, and every new member must be invited by someone already here.

The rules that protect you

The paper above is maths. Below is what it means — the rules that hold this community together, written so anyone can follow them and think about whether they're right. These are all live in the system today.

1
Credits only move — they're never created or destroyed
Every credit one person spends, another person earns. Each new member starts with 5 credits so they can receive help straight away. After that, no credits are ever created or destroyed — they only move between people. There is no pot of money. There is no inflation. Nobody can print credits.
Why this matters: Most money systems fail because someone — a bank, a government, an algorithm — can create money from thin air, diluting what everyone else holds. Here, if your balance goes up by 3, someone else's went down by 3. The community total only changes when a new member joins (adding their 5). You could add up every balance right now and check.
2
Everyone has a floor at −10
You can go into debt — but only to −10 credits. That's the furthest negative your balance can ever go. Starting from 5 credits, the most anyone can receive without giving back is 15 credits' worth of help. That's the maximum cost of trusting one person.
Why this matters: New members need to be able to receive help before they've given any — that's the whole point. But without a floor, one bad actor could take endlessly. With it, the worst case is known in advance: 15 credits. That's a cost the community has decided is worth paying to stay open. And once someone hits the floor, they can't receive any more help until they give some.
3
Membership is invite-only and invites are earned
Every new member must be invited by someone already in the community. You start with 2 invites. You earn one more for every 3 exchanges you complete, up to a maximum of 5. New members also go through a profile review before they're activated.
Why this matters: The biggest risk to a system like this is fake accounts. If someone could create 20 accounts, they could take 20 × 15 = 300 credits and disappear. Invite-only admission stops that — you need a real person to vouch for you, and that person only has a few invites to give. The invite chain creates accountability: if someone you invited causes problems, it reflects on you.
4
Once work is confirmed, it can't be cancelled
When an exchange is accepted and one person confirms the work happened, neither side can cancel. The exchange runs to completion. If there's a genuine disagreement, it goes through dispute resolution — not the cancel button.
Why this matters: Imagine doing your neighbour's gardening and then they just cancel the exchange. The system would be broken regardless of what the maths says. This rule is simple: once you confirm the work happened, you're committed. It protects the person who did the work.
5
Credits are not money
Credits are internal bookkeeping — a way to keep track of who's helped and who's been helped. They can't be converted to cash, they don't earn interest, and they can't be traded outside the community. There is no issuer, no bank, and no financial product.
Why this matters: The moment credits become tradeable for cash or earn a return, you've recreated money — with all the speculation, hoarding, and regulatory complexity that comes with it. Credits exist to encourage real exchanges between real neighbours, not to sit in accounts earning yield. They're useful inside the community and meaningless outside it. That's by design.
6
Cooperation has to be worth it — honestly
The system only works if being part of the community is genuinely better than going it alone. The paper works through this mathematically: the value you get from the community has to outweigh the temptation to take and leave. If it doesn't, the honest answer is that something needs to change — not that people should try harder.
Why this matters: Most community projects fail not from fraud but from drift — slowly providing less value until people stop showing up. The protocol is designed so that when things feel less valuable, limits tighten naturally (the temptation to leave rises, so the system gives people less rope). And if we genuinely can't make this worth your time, we'd rather face that honestly than pretend everything is fine.

Where this is going

Everything above is live today. The Cell Protocol also specifies what comes next — each upgrade designed to preserve the safety rules while expanding what the community can do.

Done: Exchange matching
Post offers and requests, propose exchanges with credits, confirm completion, balance warnings, cancel protection. Individual ledger with debt floor. Invite-only admission with profile review.
Next: Shared funds, member exit, and gradual trust
Community accounts for shared resources — a welcome fund for new members, a meal rota fund, a repair budget. Each with its own floor and ceiling, following the same conservation rules as personal balances. A formal exit procedure so when members leave (or can't continue), balances are wound down cleanly. Plus gradual trust-building: newcomers start with lower limits that grow with participation.
Then: Commitments and scheduling
Escrowed commitments for recurring obligations — meal rotas, maintenance shifts, regular helping arrangements. The bridge from exchange platform to coordination platform: the system that lets the meitheal plan, not just trade.
Then: Emergency mode
Automatic tightening when things get rough — lower limits, restricted trade, essential-services-first. And deliberate, evidence-based loosening when things improve. The system adapts to conditions rather than relying on someone to notice.
Later: Federation
Connecting with neighbouring communities for things we can't provide ourselves. Trade between communities with capped exposure — if a connection breaks, the loss is small and known in advance. Each community stays independent.

None of this is inevitable. Every step only happens if it's genuinely useful. The protocol exists so that when we do take a step, we've thought through what could go wrong — and built in the protections before we need them.
