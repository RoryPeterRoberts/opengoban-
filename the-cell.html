The Cell Protocol

The formal rules behind how our community exchange works — and where it's going.

Formal Paper (PDF)
Full proofs, corrections, and implementation plan · 17 pages

The paper above is maths. Below is what it means — the principles that hold this community together, written so anyone can follow them and think about whether they're right.

1
Nothing is created from nothing
Every credit that one person spends, another person earns. The total across the whole community is always exactly zero. There is no pot of money. There is no inflation. Nobody can print credits.
Why this matters: Most money systems fail because someone — a bank, a government, an algorithm — can create money from thin air. That dilutes what everyone else holds. In our system, credits only move. They never appear or disappear. If your balance goes up by 3, someone else's went down by 3. This can be verified at any time by anyone: add up every balance in the community. The answer is zero. Always.
2
Everyone has a floor
You can go into debt — but only so far. There's a limit to how negative your balance can go. This means someone can take from the community, but the damage is capped and known in advance.
Why this matters: New members need to be able to receive help before they've given any — that's the whole point. But without a floor, one bad actor could take endlessly. The floor means the worst anyone can do is bounded. We know the maximum cost of trusting someone, and we've decided it's a cost worth paying to keep the community open.
3
Shared funds have rules too
When the community pools credits for something — a shared meal fund, a repair budget, a tool library — those pots follow the same rules as personal balances. They have floors and ceilings. They can't create credit. Every movement in or out has a matching movement somewhere else.
Why this matters: Shared pots are where corruption usually starts. A committee controls a fund, and slowly the fund becomes a way to move value to friends. By giving community funds hard limits and requiring every transfer to have a counterparty, we make this kind of capture visible and bounded. Even if someone takes over governance and tries to drain a fund, they hit the floor.
4
Trust is earned slowly
New members start with a low credit limit. It grows over time based on participation — but slowly. There's a maximum speed at which your limit can increase, no matter what.
Why this matters: The most dangerous attack on a community like this is patience. Someone creates a few fake identities, behaves well for months, then takes everything at once. Slow trust-building is the defence. Even with three fake identities running for six months, the maximum damage is about three weeks of one person's labour. That's survivable. And it required six months of actually helping people — at which point, were they really faking?
5
Connections between communities are limited
If our community trades with a neighbouring one, there's a cap on how much we can be owed or owe at any time. If that connection breaks, we lose at most a small, known fraction of our total capacity.
Why this matters: This is how financial crises spread — one failure cascades through connections. We prevent that by limiting exposure per link and in total. Even if every external connection failed at once, the damage is bounded. Communities can trade without risking their survival on each other's stability.
6
Bad times tighten the rules — and good times loosen them
If the community is under stress — too many people leaving, essential services failing — the system automatically tightens: lower credit limits, restricted trade, essential-services-first scheduling. When things improve, these restrictions lift gradually.
Why this matters: Crises make people more likely to defect. If the ship looks like it's sinking, the rational move is to take what you can and leave. Tighter rules during bad times reduce the payoff from defecting. But we also need a way back to normal — otherwise one bad week means permanently operating under emergency rules. The return to normal is deliberately slow: things must be good for several weeks before restrictions ease, and they ease gradually. This prevents the cycle of crisis, relaxation, and immediate re-crisis.
7
Once work is confirmed, it can't be cancelled
When an exchange is in progress and one person has confirmed they did their part, the other person can no longer cancel. If someone does the work, the system protects them.
Why this matters: Real communities run on the belief that effort will be recognised. If someone can do their neighbour's gardening and then the neighbour just cancels, the system is broken regardless of what the maths says. This rule is simple: if you confirmed the work happened, you're committed. Disputes go through dispute resolution, not the cancel button.
8
Cooperation must be worth it
The whole system only works if being part of the community is genuinely better than going it alone. We track two things: how confident members are that the community will persist, and how much value the community actually provides. If either falls too far, the honest answer is that the community should dissolve rather than pretend everything is fine.
Why this matters: Most community projects fail not from fraud but from drift — slowly providing less value until people stop showing up. By measuring confidence and delivery, we get an early warning. And by having an honest dissolution condition, we avoid the trap of communities that exist on paper long after they stopped working. If we can't make this worth your time, we'd rather tell you that than waste it.

Where this is going

Connect Again today is the starting point — a simple exchange board where neighbours help each other. The Cell Protocol is the foundation we're building toward: a fully specified system for communities that can provide for their members reliably, even under pressure.

Now: Exchange matching
Neighbours post offers and needs. Propose exchanges, agree on credits, confirm completion. Simple individual balances with a basic debt floor.
Next: Household accounts and shared resources
Families participate as households. Community meal rotas with proper credit settlement. Shared repair fund and tool library. The building blocks of a community that can actually provide essentials.
Then: Viability and resilience
Can we cover the essential roles? Do we have the skills we need? What happens if things get hard? Emergency planning, dispute resolution with real timelines, and the honest question: are we viable?
Later: Federation
Connecting with neighbouring communities for things we can't provide ourselves. Trade with bounded risk. Each community stays independent and self-governing.

None of this is inevitable. Every step only happens if it's genuinely useful. The protocol exists so that when we do take a step, we've thought through what could go wrong — and built in the protections before we need them.
